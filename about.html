About

I started where emergencies happen. Now I build the tools to prepare for them.

I'm Ahmed Sorour — public health and disaster medicine specialist at WHO Egypt, focused on emergency care. Building AI-augmented platforms for disaster preparedness from Cairo.

The three arcs

Most careers in global health follow a single trajectory. Mine has three — and the thread connecting them is the same question I keep asking: why don't the people doing the hardest work have the tools they need?

The frontline (2005 — 2018)

I started on the frontline. First as an offshore doctor on deep-sea drilling rigs in the Mediterranean — the Maersk Discoverer, then the Egyptian Drilling Company's El Qaher 1. Four years of making clinical decisions in complete medical isolation, where the nearest hospital was a helicopter flight away and the medevac decision was yours alone.

Then emergency care training at Alexandria University Hospitals — high-volume, undifferentiated presentations, the kind of experience that teaches you to think fast with incomplete information. Those years built two convictions: systems matter more than individual heroics, and the tools people use in emergencies are almost always designed by people who've never been in one.

The systems (2018 — present)

I joined WHO Egypt in 2020, initially managing the COVID-19 UN isolation facility — direct patient care for UN personnel in a community-based setting. Then the work shifted to where I could have the most impact: emergency care implementation and disaster preparedness at national scale.

Today I lead the national rollout of WHO's Basic Emergency Care programme in partnership with the African Federation for Emergency Medicine. I coordinate mass casualty management and hospital resilience initiatives. I support disaster response planning across national and subnational levels — including surge planning during the Sudan and Gaza crises.

In parallel, I advise on complex international clinical cases at Bupa Global, keeping one foot in direct clinical reasoning while the other works at systems level.

The building (2023 — present)

Somewhere along the way, I started building. I watched simulation exercises run on paper injects and WhatsApp groups. I watched health facility assessments generate data that lived in spreadsheets no one revisited. I watched training programmes produce certificates but struggle to measure whether they actually changed practice.

So I built tools to fix that. SimReady is an AI-augmented simulation exercise platform — 11 releases shipped, 105 API endpoints, 12 exercise classes, 22 countries, offline-first with full Arabic RTL support. The National Assessment Platform consolidates WHO-aligned facility assessment tools into a single digital framework.

Both are in active development. Both trace back to specific failures I watched happen in the field.

What I believe

The tools haven't caught up with the people.

The people running disaster responses, managing mass casualty events, and training health workers in emergency care are doing extraordinary work with inadequate tools. Paper-based simulation exercises. Fragmented assessment data. Evaluation by memory. The gap isn't skill or commitment — it's infrastructure.

AI should suggest. Humans should decide. The platform's job is to make that boundary clear in every interaction — not to replace clinical judgement, but to give practitioners the information and structure they need to exercise it well.

And this gets built from Cairo. The talent here is real, the problems are proximate, and the solutions have to work offline, in Arabic, in a field hospital with unreliable connectivity. That constraint isn't a limitation — it's a design advantage.
